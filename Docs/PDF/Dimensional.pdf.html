Abstract—In the late 80s, Ackoff ﬁrst proposed a cognitive
category upon the content of human mind, which included
Data, Information, Knowledge and Wisdom, or DIKW. Since
then, the DIKW hierarchy has attracted a ﬂurry of further
research studies. Its applications include ontology composition,
decision making theory, domain speciﬁc engineering theory,
software design thought process automation, etc., to name a
few.
Partially based on our previous work on human centric
situation studies, we propose a dimensional situation analytics
towards a new view on the DIKW hierarchy. By incorporating
functional MapReduce computing paradigm, we present in
this paper a novel (MR)2 paradigm, which refers to two
consecutive MapReduce that cut across the boundaries between
Data, Information, Knowledge and Wisdom. We argue that
our (MR)2 paradigm promotes comprehensive decision making
and therefore, offers new insights in knowledge transformation
from data to wisdom.
Keywords: Situ framework, Situation theory, Ontology, Sit-
uation Analytics, Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom
(DIKW), MapReduce.
1. Introduction and Related Work
Back in the 1980s, Ackoff [1] ﬁrst proposed a cogni-
tive category upon the content of the human mind, which
included Data, Information, Knowledge and Wisdom, a.k.a
“DIKW Hierarchy”. Since then, it has been applied in var-
ious research areas, and most existing work can be divided
into two categories:
1) Discussion of the concepts of DIKW at the philo-
sophical level [30] [33] [19] [9];
2) Application of DIKW to solve a speciﬁc computa-
tional problem [15] [35] [36].
The success of sharing expert designer’s thought pro-
cess across the entire design team oftentimes contributes
signiﬁcantly to the quality, process productivity and timely
completion of non-trivial, industrial strength software engi-
neering projects. The communication conveyed through tra-
ditional software design documents falls short of effectively
sharing design expertise [3] [32]. The expression of common
sense, the vast diversity of domain speciﬁc knowledge of
experts, and the epistemic-level interplay between experts’
knowledge base and the decision making process involved
in solving speciﬁc design problems all make the sharing of
experts’ thought process truly difﬁcult. Even more challeng-
ing is to mechanize such sharings. Meeting the challenge of
automating design wisdom sharing will be of great value to
large-scale software engineering projects.
Oyama et al [31] proposed a CAPIS Model based
software design method to share experts’ thought pro-
cess. It adopts a Data, Information, Knowledge and Wis-
dom (DIKW) hierarchy [1] [10] to embrace Knowledge-
hierarchical Conceptulization (KC), an ontology that weaves
in wisdom from design experts. The combination of a
Problem Issues Solution (PIS) Ontological Mapping with
Knowledge-hierarchical Conceptulization presents a Causal-
effect decision making support [37], alongside with effective
communication of design rationales [24], in software design
process. Therefore, they name their methodology CAusality
of Problem-Issues-Solution (CAPIS).
Unfortunately, despite all these good pieces of work,
there is a gap between the conceptualization of DIKW at
the philosophical level and the application of DIKW to solve
speciﬁc computational problems. This is because the former
is too high-level, and not computable, and the latter is too
speciﬁc, and problem-oriented (mostly statistical analysis
problems). Hence, none of the existing work can provide
a generally feasible, theoretically computable framework to
learn from historical data.
Many studies have been published to address the data
learning problems for various software engineering related
purposes. One of the branches is to research on how to
use data analytic techniques to improve software itself, i.e.
to elicit initial system requirements [14] [12], or to evolve
software [11] [22] [2].

2.1. Background and Prior Work on Situ
Situ framework proposes a correspondence theory be-
tween user’s human intention and the observed temporal
sequence of situations towards achieving a common goal.
As described in [13] [27] [28], in the context of system goals
previously used in Goal-Oriented Requirement Engineering
[38], [39],
the goals deﬁned under Situ mirror human
desires. In other words, the state in the domain of software
development reﬂects that of the invisible mental states of
a human user. The core concept
in Situ framework is
situation. Conceptually, a situation is described with regard
to its context surroundings and human mental states:
situation ∼ (d, A, E)t
A and E stand for behavioral and environmental con-
texts respectively, whereas d represents human desire, at a
particular time point t.
In Situ, each situation must contain at least one action.
Each action comprises certain behavioral contexts of the
situation. Each situation exists within an environment com-
posed of contextual data. Key observations regarding user
action is that a user’s action with related behavioral contexts
is a direct embodiment of her or his desires; moreover, a
user’s action also closely interacts with their environmental
contexts.
With an ultimate objective of shortening the evolution
cycle of software services, Situ [13] takes an analytical
approach to present a context-oriented, human-centric situ-
ation theory. It is a uniﬁed framework that embraces data
analytics, through behavioral and environmental context data
handling, and human’s epistemological analysis by directly
including human desire as part of a situation. From the
perspective of DIKW hierarchy, we apply the situation
theory Situ initiated as a catalyst to facilitate the work ﬂow
from data, to information and knowledge, which eventually
contributes to the theoretical analysis of the human decision
making process.

3. Conclusion and Future Work
This paper presents a model of dimensional situation an-
alytics towards an objective architecture where the hierarchy
of Data, Information, Knowledge and Wisdom are seam-
lessly incorporated. A new situation-theoretic approach has
been proposed to conduct the entire situation analysis over
DIKW. The map reduce paradigm as a built-in mechanism
in our approach makes it especially suitable to automate the
task that fuses data, information and knowledge into human
wisdom.
We plan to continue exploring the possibility to apply the
theory presented in this paper as an ontology-based support
to real life human-centric computing, which will further
indicate the effectiveness of our approach. [30].
